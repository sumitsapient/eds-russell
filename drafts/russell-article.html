Jobs Slow, Trade Talks Enter New Phase — Market Week in Review

By Andrew Pease, Chief Investment Strategist  |  July 7, 2026

U.S. labor market momentum continued to ease in June, as non-farm payrolls came in below consensus expectations for the second consecutive month. At the same time, diplomatic channels between the United States and its major trading partners showed renewed activity, signaling that negotiations on tariffs and market access have entered a more substantive phase. For investors, these developments carry distinct implications across asset classes.

Jobs Data: Cooling Without Cracking

June non-farm payrolls rose by 142,000 — modestly below the Bloomberg consensus of 165,000. The unemployment rate held steady at 4.1%, while average hourly earnings grew 3.8% year-over-year. That figure remains above the level consistent with the Federal Reserve's 2% inflation target, but the trend is clearly softening. Revisions to April and May reduced the cumulative two-month total by 58,000 jobs, reinforcing the picture of a labor market that is normalizing rather than deteriorating.

The sectoral breakdown was notable. Goods-producing industries — particularly manufacturing and construction — shed jobs for the third straight month, consistent with the dampening effect of elevated tariff uncertainty on business investment. Services hiring remained the primary driver of headline growth, though the pace in leisure and hospitality, health care, and professional services all moderated from their first-quarter averages. The household survey showed a modest uptick in part-time employment for economic reasons, a leading indicator the Fed will be watching closely.

Trade Talks Enter a New Phase

After months of largely symbolic exchanges, the U.S. and its primary trading counterparts appear to have moved from positional signaling to substantive negotiation. Delegations from the U.S. and China met in Geneva for a second round of working-group discussions, with both sides describing the talks as "constructive." Separately, a framework agreement with the European Union on critical minerals and technology supply chains was initialed on Thursday, providing a template that could be applied more broadly.

The shift in tone matters because it reduces the tail risk of further tariff escalation that has weighed on capital expenditure decisions across global manufacturers. Markets have largely priced in a "contained uncertainty" scenario — not a full rollback of existing tariffs, but an implicit truce that prevents conditions from worsening. Any tangible progress toward permanent tariff relief would likely be an upside catalyst, particularly for export-oriented equities in Europe and emerging markets.

Market Implications: What We Are Watching

From a multi-asset perspective, the combination of cooling labor data and thawing trade relations creates a nuanced backdrop. U.S. equity markets remain supported by resilient corporate earnings and a Fed that is increasingly positioned to ease later this year — our base case is one 25 basis-point cut in Q4 2026. However, valuations in large-cap U.S. growth remain elevated relative to history, leaving the market with limited margin of safety should the economic data deteriorate faster than expected.

In fixed income, the 2-year/10-year spread has steepened modestly following the jobs report, reflecting market expectations that the Fed's next move is a cut rather than a hold. Investment-grade credit spreads remain tight. We continue to see value in short-to-intermediate duration investment-grade bonds as a ballast against equity volatility, while maintaining our underweight to long-duration Treasuries given the structural deficit backdrop.

"The labor market is telling us the economy is slowing to a more sustainable pace — not falling off a cliff. Trade talks moving from theater to substance removes one source of uncertainty that has been holding back business investment. Combined, these are manageable signals for a diversified portfolio." — Andrew Pease, Chief Investment Strategist

Key Takeaways for Investors

  • Labor market: Gradual cooling, not deterioration. Watch revisions and part-time employment trends as leading indicators.
  • Trade: Dialogue has become more substantive. A formal tariff rollback remains a potential upside surprise for equities.
  • U.S. equities: Large-cap growth valuations are full. International diversification offers relative value at current levels.
  • Fixed income: Short-to-intermediate investment-grade remains our preferred defensive allocation.
  • Federal Reserve: Base case: one 25 bps cut in Q4 2026. Labor market softening and core PCE trajectory will determine the pace.
